# Narrative Builder

## When to use
- You have the analysis and now need it to *land* with a busy executive audience.
- A recommendation must survive a hostile room (board, investment committee, skeptical CEO).
- You're building a deck/memo and the logic is there but the story isn't sharp.

## When not to use
- You need the one-page written artifact specifically → use `decision-memo`.
- The recommendation itself isn't decided → finish `strategic-options` first.

## Method
1. **Structure with the Pyramid Principle.** One governing thought (the answer) at the top, supported by 3–4 mutually-exclusive arguments, each backed by evidence. Every level *answers the question raised by the level above*. Lead with the answer — executives want the conclusion first, the build second (this is the opposite of how the analysis was done).
2. **Open with SCQA.** Situation (agreed context) → Complication (what changed / the tension) → Question (the decision) → Answer (your recommendation). This makes the "why now" unavoidable and earns the next 10 minutes.
3. **Make each supporting argument MECE and "so-what"-driven.** Each is a complete thought (a sentence with a verb and a point of view), not a topic label. "Revenue" is a label; "Growth has stalled because expansion collapsed, not because acquisition slowed" is an argument.
4. **Build the Day-1 / governing-thought slide and four lengths.** A single slide/sentence that carries the whole recommendation, then 30-second, 2-minute, 10-minute, and 60-minute versions. You won't get the time you planned for — be ready for all four.
5. **Prepare the hostile questions before the room asks.** List the 6–10 sharpest objections, the answer to each, and the evidence behind it. The questions you fear most are the ones to rehearse most.

## Decision rules
- Answer first, always. Burying the recommendation under build-up loses an executive audience in the first minute.
- Each supporting point is an assertion with a point of view, not a category. If it has no verb, it's not an argument.
- If you can't say it in 30 seconds, you don't have the story yet — the long versions are expansions of the short one, not different stories.

## Output format
1. **Governing thought** — the one-sentence answer.
2. **Pyramid** — the 3–4 supporting arguments (each a full assertion) with evidence under each.
3. **SCQA opening** — the four lines that set up the recommendation.
4. **Four lengths** — 30s / 2m / 10m / 60m versions, each self-contained.
5. **Hostile-question prep** — the toughest objections with crisp, evidenced answers.

## Quality bar
- Answer-first; the governing thought is stated before the build.
- Supporting points are assertions with a point of view, MECE — not topic headings.
- Tight, specific language; no filler, no hedging, no throat-clearing ("In today's dynamic landscape…").
- Hostile questions are genuinely hard and honestly answered, not softballs.

## Common failure modes
- Chronological build-up ("here's everything we did…") that buries the answer.
- Section headers ("Market," "Financials") masquerading as arguments.
- Generic management-speak that could front any deck and commits to nothing.

## Pairs with
Consumes the recommendation from `strategic-options` / `decision-memo` and the evidence from the analysis skills. Feeds `stakeholder-alignment` (the message to pre-wire) and `decision-memo` (the written form). Use the `design-guide` to choose how each argument is visualized.

## Worked example
Input: a recommendation to exit a legacy product line. Governing thought: "Exit the legacy line within 12 months and redeploy the capital to the cloud segment — it raises group ROIC by ~4 points." SCQA: Situation (legacy is 30% of revenue), Complication (its pool is draining 9%/yr and it consumes a third of capital), Question (keep investing, harvest, or exit?), Answer (exit and redeploy). Three MECE supports: (1) the profit pool is structurally migrating away; (2) we have no right to win it back; (3) the capital earns 3× the return in cloud. 30-second version leads with the ROIC number. Hostile-question prep includes "what about the customers and the brand?" and "why not just harvest?" — each answered with the pool-migration and capital-productivity evidence.
