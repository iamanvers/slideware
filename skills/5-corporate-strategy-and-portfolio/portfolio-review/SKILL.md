# Portfolio Review

## When to use
- A multi-business / multi-product / multi-bet portfolio is funded by inertia ("last year + a bit").
- You suspect strong units are starved while weak ones are subsidized.
- A new leader/board wants an evidence-based reallocation, not across-the-board cuts.

## When not to use
- You're choosing a single direction for one business → use `strategic-options`.
- You're ranking discrete projects within one budget → use `initiative-prioritizer`.
- The question is the corporate-level allocation framework/process → use `capital-allocation`.

## Method
1. **Place each unit on the GE–McKinsey matrix:** market attractiveness (size, growth, margin, structure) × competitive strength (relative share, cost position, capability). Use a weighted score per axis, not gut feel, so placements are defensible.
2. **Overlay capital and returns.** For each unit show capital consumed, ROIC vs cost of capital, and growth. This exposes the two pathologies: **starved winners** (high attractiveness/strength, under-funded) and **subsidized laggards** (low/low, still fed).
3. **Assign a posture:** Grow (fund aggressively), Hold (fund to maintain), Fix (turnaround with a deadline and exit trigger), or Exit/Harvest (stop investing, extract cash, or divest).
4. **Reallocate explicitly.** State what gets *more*, what gets *less*, and what *stops* — with the dollar moves. Reallocation that doesn't move real money is theater.
5. **Set the review cadence and triggers.** When is each posture revisited, and what result would force a change (especially the Fix deadline).

## Key formulas / lenses
- Economic value added per unit = (ROIC − WACC) × invested capital. Negative EVA that isn't a deliberate seed bet is a candidate to fix or exit.
- Capital productivity = growth or profit generated per $ of capital consumed; rank units by it to find starved winners.
- "Cash trap" test: a unit consuming cash with ROIC < WACC and no credible path above it → harvest or exit.

## Decision rules
- Fund the winners first. Spreading capital evenly ("peanut butter") starves the units that could compound and props up those that can't.
- Every Fix needs a deadline and a pre-committed exit trigger; a permanent turnaround is an exit in denial.
- Sunk cost and heritage are not arguments. Decide on forward economics and right-to-win only.

## Output format
1. **Portfolio matrix** — units on attractiveness × strength, bubble size = capital or revenue.
2. **Capital/returns overlay** — table: unit | capital | ROIC vs WACC | growth | EVA.
3. **Posture per unit** — Grow / Hold / Fix / Exit, with the rationale.
4. **Reallocation** — the explicit dollar moves: more / less / stop.
5. **Governance** — review cadence and the trigger to revisit each posture (Fix deadlines especially).

## Quality bar
- Axis placements are scored and defensible, not vibes on a 2×2.
- Capital and returns are overlaid — a matrix without the money is decoration.
- Output moves real dollars and names something to *stop*; "continue to monitor" everything is a non-decision.
- Fixes carry deadlines and exit triggers.

## Common failure modes
- A pretty 2×2 with no capital overlay and no actual reallocation.
- Protecting heritage/loss-making units on sentiment.
- Across-the-board cuts/raises instead of differentiated reallocation.

## Pairs with
Uses `market-mapping` (attractiveness), `competitive-intel`/`profit-pool-analysis` (strength), and `valuation` (what an exited unit is worth). Sits under `capital-allocation` (the corporate framework) and feeds `transformation-roadmap` for the units being fixed.

## Worked example
Input: 8 business lines funded roughly flat each year. Scored placement plus a capital/returns overlay reveals two high-attractiveness/high-strength lines running ROIC of 25%+ but capital-starved (capital productivity double the portfolio average), while one low/low line has consumed cash for three years at ROIC < WACC with no path up. Postures: Grow the two starved winners (+$40M reallocated), Hold three, Fix two on a 12-month deadline with an exit trigger, Exit the structurally weak line (divest, redeploy proceeds). Net: capital shifts from sentiment-funded laggards to compounding winners; one line exits.
