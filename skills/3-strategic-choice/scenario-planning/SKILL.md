# Scenario Planning

## When to use
- The future is genuinely uncertain over a long horizon (regulation, technology shift, geopolitics, demand structure) and a single forecast would be false precision.
- You're making a big, hard-to-reverse bet and need to know whether it holds across futures.
- Leadership is planning off one base case and you want to stress that complacency.

## When not to use
- The uncertainty is adversarial/competitive reactions → use `war-gaming`.
- You're testing the assumptions inside one plan → use `assumption-audit`.

## Method
1. **Define the focal question and horizon.** The decision the scenarios must inform and the time frame (usually 3–10 years). Scenarios serve a decision; they are not futurology for its own sake.
2. **Identify driving forces, then isolate the critical uncertainties.** List the forces shaping the outcome (STEEP: social, technological, economic, environmental, political). Separate the **predetermined** (largely knowable trends — demographics, locked-in tech) from the **critical uncertainties** (high-impact + high-uncertainty). The two most important, most independent uncertainties become your axes.
3. **Build 2–4 distinct scenarios on a 2×2** of the two critical uncertainties. Each scenario is internally consistent, plausible, and *materially different*; give each a memorable name and a short narrative of how the world got there.
4. **Stress-test the strategy in each scenario.** For each future: does the current/proposed strategy win, survive, or break? What new opportunities and threats appear? This reveals which bets are fragile and which are robust.
5. **Derive robust + contingent moves and early indicators.** **No-regret moves** that pay off in every scenario; **options/hedges** that buy the right to act if a scenario emerges; and **signposts** — observable early indicators that tell you which scenario is materializing, so you can shift before competitors.

## Decision rules
- Scenarios must be plausible and distinct, not best/base/worst of the same story (that's sensitivity analysis, not scenario planning). Vary the *structure* of the world, not just a number.
- Assign no probabilities-as-truth; the value is robustness across futures, not predicting the "right" one.
- The output is *decisions* (no-regret moves, hedges, signposts), not four interesting stories. A scenario set that changes no decision failed.

## Output format
1. **Focal question & horizon** — the decision and the time frame.
2. **Driving forces** — predetermined elements vs critical uncertainties; the two chosen axes.
3. **Scenario set** — 2–4 named, narrated futures on the 2×2, each internally consistent.
4. **Strategy stress-test** — how the strategy fares (win/survive/break) in each, with opportunities/threats.
5. **Robust & contingent moves** — no-regret actions, hedges/options, and the signposts to monitor.

## Quality bar
- Scenarios vary the structure of the world and are genuinely distinct — not high/mid/low of one forecast.
- Built from explicit critical uncertainties, with predetermined trends separated out.
- Ends in decisions and signposts that change what you do now, not just narratives.
- Each scenario is plausible and internally consistent — no caricatures.

## Common failure modes
- Best/base/worst case relabeled as scenarios (it's just sensitivity on one number).
- Vivid stories with no link to any decision.
- Implicitly betting on the "most likely" scenario, defeating the purpose.

## Pairs with
Complements `strategic-options` (test options across futures) and `assumption-audit` (the critical uncertainties often *are* the load-bearing assumptions). Signposts feed `kpi-architect`/`risk-mitigation`; robust moves feed `transformation-roadmap`.

## Worked example
Input: a utility deciding on a 10-year generation investment. Two critical uncertainties: pace of energy-transition policy (slow ↔ aggressive) and electricity demand growth (flat ↔ surging from electrification). Four scenarios: "Slow & Flat," "Green Boom" (aggressive policy + surging demand), "Stranded" (aggressive policy + flat demand — assets stranded), "Muddle Through." The proposed gas-plant bet wins in two, breaks in "Stranded." No-regret move: invest in grid flexibility and storage (pays in all four). Hedge: take an option on renewable capacity rather than committing now. Signposts: carbon-price trajectory and EV-adoption rate — monitored quarterly to tell which world is arriving before the capital is locked.
