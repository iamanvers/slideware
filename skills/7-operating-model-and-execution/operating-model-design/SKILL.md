# Operating Model Design

## When to use
- A strategy is set but the organization isn't built to deliver it.
- A new business model (e.g., product → solutions, or a new segment) needs different capabilities and decision rights.
- Accountability is blurry: decisions stall, hand-offs break, "everyone and no one" owns key outcomes.

## When not to use
- You're only redrawing the org chart for spans/layers/cost → that's the structural slice; do it here but driven by capabilities, not boxes first.
- You're sequencing the change over time → use `transformation-roadmap`.

## Method
1. **Derive the required capabilities from the strategy.** Work backwards: this strategy wins only if we are genuinely good at *these 4–6 things*. Distinguish capabilities that must be **differentiating** (build/own, world-class) from **table-stakes** (be adequate, can outsource). Don't try to be great at everything.
2. **Design structure to own those capabilities.** Choose the primary axis (function / business unit / geography / segment / product) based on where the critical decisions and customer interface sit. Name where the matrix tensions will be and how they're resolved.
3. **Assign decision rights with RAPID or RACI.** For each major recurring decision, name who Recommends, Agrees, Performs, has Input, and Decides (RAPID) — or Responsible/Accountable/Consulted/Informed (RACI). Exactly one Decider/Accountable per decision. Ambiguous decision rights are the #1 cause of slow execution.
4. **Map the core processes and the breaking hand-offs.** Lay out the value-creating processes end to end and flag the cross-boundary hand-offs most likely to fail (where accountability passes between units). Design the interface, SLA, or shared metric that holds them.
5. **Check coherence across all elements.** Strategy → capabilities → structure → decision rights → processes → metrics/incentives must reinforce each other. Incentives that reward the old behavior will defeat the new structure every time.

## Decision rules
- Capabilities drive structure, never the reverse. Designing boxes before capabilities produces a reorg that changes names and nothing else.
- One accountable owner per decision and per outcome. If you can't name one, the design isn't finished.
- Match the operating model to the strategy's logic: efficiency strategies favor centralization/standardization; responsiveness/innovation strategies favor decentralization/autonomy. A mismatch quietly kills the strategy.

## Output format
1. **Required capability set** — the 4–6 capabilities, each tagged differentiating vs table-stakes, with the current gap.
2. **Structure** — the org design and its primary axis, with the rationale and named matrix tensions.
3. **Decision-rights map** — RAPID/RACI for the major recurring decisions.
4. **Process & hand-off map** — core processes and the high-risk hand-offs with their interface design.
5. **Coherence check** — how incentives/metrics reinforce (not fight) the new model, and what must change.

## Quality bar
- Capabilities are derived from *this* strategy, not a generic list ("be agile, be data-driven").
- Decision rights are explicit with a single decider each — not a vague RACI with three "accountables."
- Incentive/coherence check included — most reorgs fail on incentives, not boxes.
- Names the breaking hand-offs specifically; "improve collaboration" is not a design.

## Common failure modes
- Boxes-first reorg that renames the chart without changing capabilities or decision rights.
- Copying a structure from another company whose strategy was different.
- Leaving incentives pointed at the old behavior so the new model never takes.

## Pairs with
Follows `strategic-options` (the chosen strategy). Pairs with `transformation-roadmap` (sequence the change), `kpi-architect` (metrics that reinforce the model), `stakeholder-alignment` (reorgs are political), and `cost-transformation` (if structure is also a cost lever).

## Worked example
Input: a product company shifting to solutions selling. Required capabilities: industry solution design, outcome-based pricing, post-sale value management, and partner orchestration — the first three are differentiating (build), partner ops is table-stakes (can outsource). Structure shifts from product-line silos to industry-aligned pods with a shared solutions platform. RAPID for pricing: pod GM Decides, finance Agrees, solution architect Recommends — replacing the old committee where no one owned it. Highest-risk hand-off: sales → delivery at contract signature; fixed with a shared "value realized" metric both are paid on. Coherence fix: change sales comp from license bookings to multi-year solution value, or the old behavior persists.
