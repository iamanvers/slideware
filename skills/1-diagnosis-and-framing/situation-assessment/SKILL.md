# Situation Assessment

## When to use
- The opening move of almost any engagement: you need the factual baseline before choosing a direction.
- Leadership has asked a question but you suspect it is the wrong question, or a symptom dressed as a cause.
- A new owner, board, or executive needs a fast, honest read of "where are we, really."

## When not to use
- The problem is already framed and agreed → go straight to `problem-structuring`.
- You already have the baseline and need to find the bottleneck → use `growth-barriers`.

## Method
1. **Frame with SCQA.** Write the Situation (stable context everyone accepts), the Complication (what changed and why it now demands a decision), the Question (the single decision question), and your Answer-hypothesis (the working answer to disprove). If you cannot state the Question in one sentence, you are not ready to analyze.
2. **Triangulate performance across three lenses.** Financial (growth rate, gross/operating margin, cash conversion, returns on capital), Market (share and share trajectory, demand growth, relative positioning, NPS/retention), Operational (throughput, quality/defects, cost-to-serve, utilization). For each metric show the **level, the trend, and the benchmark** — a number with no trend or comparator is not yet a finding.
3. **Build a MECE issue tree** from observed symptoms down to candidate root causes. Tag every branch as `[FACT]` (sourced), `[INFERENCE]` (reasoned from facts), or `[UNKNOWN]` (must be tested). The tag distribution itself is a finding — mostly inference means you are guessing.
4. **Separate signal from noise on a 2×2** of *impact on the decision* vs *certainty of the evidence*. High-impact/high-certainty = act now; high-impact/low-certainty = these are the analyses to run next; low-impact = drop, however loud.
5. **State the real question back.** Confirm or reframe the question leadership asked. Naming the right question is the single highest-leverage output of this step.

## Decision rules
- A "finding" must carry level + trend + comparator. Otherwise it is data, not a finding.
- If >50% of the issue tree is `[INFERENCE]` or `[UNKNOWN]`, the honest deliverable is a workplan to close gaps, not a recommendation.
- Reframe the question whenever the asked question presumes a cause ("how do we cut price?") rather than naming the decision ("how do we restore margin?").

## Output format
1. **The question** (one sentence) — confirmed or reframed, with a one-line "why this is the real question."
2. **One-page fact base** — 6–10 findings, each: metric → level/trend/benchmark → "so what."
3. **Momentum read** — is the business getting better or worse, and how fast (trajectory, not snapshot).
4. **Ranked issues list** — load-bearing issues from the 2×2, each tagged fact/inference/unknown.
5. **Open questions** — the 2–4 analyses that must close next, with the method to close each.

## Quality bar
- Every claim is sourced or explicitly tagged as inference/unknown — no confident assertions resting on nothing.
- "So what" on every finding; a fact base that reads like a metrics dump has failed.
- Trajectory over snapshot: where it is *heading* matters more than where it *is*.
- Resist the loudest symptom; the fact base should sometimes contradict the executive's framing.

## Common failure modes
- Boiling the ocean — analyzing everything instead of what moves the decision.
- Accepting the asked question without testing whether it is the right one.
- Presenting data without a "so what," or inferences dressed as facts.

## Pairs with
Feeds `problem-structuring` (turn issues into a hypothesis-driven workplan), `growth-barriers` (if the issue is growth), and `assumption-audit` (stress-test the answer-hypothesis).

## Worked example
Input: "Enterprise renewals are declining — fix our pricing." Three-lens read shows gross margin stable and price realization flat, but net revenue retention fell from 112% to 94%, concentrated entirely in accounts onboarded after a Q2 process change. Reframed question: not "how do we price?" but "why does the post-Q2 onboarding cohort fail to expand?" Two analyses named to confirm (cohort expansion curves, onboarding-step drop-off). The pricing hypothesis is graded low-certainty and parked.
