# Profit Pool Analysis

## When to use
- Revenue and profit live in different places in your industry and you need to know which stages to own.
- You're considering vertical integration, a move up/down the chain, or a business-model shift.
- A market looks crowded on revenue but you suspect the profit is concentrated somewhere specific.

## When not to use
- You need demand size and white space, not margin distribution → use `market-mapping`.
- You need to reallocate capital across *your own* business units → use `portfolio-review`.

## Method
1. **Map the full value chain** from raw input to end customer — every stage where value is added and money changes hands, including adjacent services and aftermarket, not just the obvious product step.
2. **Size revenue and profit per stage.** For each stage estimate industry revenue and operating margin → profit pool = revenue × margin. Plot stages on a chart: **width = revenue share, height = margin %, area = absolute profit**. The picture usually surprises — revenue-heavy stages are often margin-thin.
3. **Locate yourself vs the deepest pools.** Mark where your business currently captures profit against where the deepest pools sit. The gap is the strategic question.
4. **Read the migration.** Identify where the pool is *moving* — bundling/unbundling, digitization, regulation, shifts in buyer power, new entrants skimming a stage. Today's pool tells you where money is; migration tells you where to position.
5. **Derive the implication.** Move toward deep/growing pools you have a right to win; defend or harvest pools that are migrating away; avoid investing harder into a shrinking pool just because it's your heritage.

## Key formulas
- Stage profit pool = stage industry revenue × stage operating margin %.
- Your capture = your profit at a stage ÷ total stage profit pool.
- Pool concentration: % of total chain profit in the top stage (high concentration = the game is won/lost at one stage).

## Decision rules
- Follow profit, not revenue — the largest-revenue stage is frequently the lowest-margin (often deliberately commoditized by the stage that captures the pool).
- Position where the pool is *deepening*, not where it is deepest-but-eroding.
- Beware the heritage trap: being good at a stage whose pool is draining is a reason to move, not to double down.

## Output format
1. **Value-chain map** — every stage named.
2. **Profit pool chart** — width = revenue, height = margin, area = absolute profit, per stage.
3. **Your position** — where you capture profit vs where the deep pools are.
4. **Migration read** — where the pool is moving and the force driving it.
5. **Strategic implication** — where to move toward, defend, or exit, with the right-to-win check.

## Quality bar
- Profit pools sized with explicit revenue × margin per stage and sources/estimates flagged — not eyeballed.
- Distinguishes revenue from profit at every stage (the whole point of the analysis).
- Includes a migration view; a static snapshot misses the strategy.
- States right-to-win, not just where the money is — money you can't capture isn't a strategy.

## Common failure modes
- Sizing revenue and calling it profit (ignoring margin differences across stages).
- Stopping at the obvious product stage and missing aftermarket/services pools.
- Recommending a move into a deep pool the company has no right to win.

## Pairs with
Pairs with `market-mapping` (demand) and `competitive-intel` (who holds each stage). Feeds `strategic-options`, `portfolio-review`, and `commercial-due-diligence` (target's pool position).

## Worked example
Input: a hardware-led business assuming its product line is the prize. Pool chart: hardware = 60% of chain revenue but ~8% margin → a thin pool; downstream service, financing, and aftermarket parts = 25% of revenue but ~35% margin → ~70% of total chain profit. The company captures almost none of the service pool. Migration: digitization is moving even more value to recurring service/data. Implication: the strategy isn't a better box — it's an attach motion into service and aftermarket, where the pool is deep, growing, and (given the installed base) winnable.
