# Commercial Due Diligence

## When to use
- You're evaluating an acquisition, investment, or merger and need an independent read on the target's commercial reality.
- A deal thesis exists ("we're buying growth / capability / a market position") and must be validated, not assumed.
- An investment committee needs to know whether the management forecast is credible and what could break it.

## When not to use
- You're sizing the merger benefit between two known parties → use `synergy-valuation`.
- You're putting a number on the business → use `valuation` (CDD feeds it).

## Method
1. **Pin down the deal thesis and the questions that prove/disprove it.** Why are we buying this, at this price? Translate the thesis into the 4–6 commercial questions whose answers decide go/no-go (this is `problem-structuring` applied to a deal).
2. **Assess market attractiveness (outside-in).** Size and growth (`market-mapping`), structure and profit pools (`profit-pool-analysis`), and the durability of growth — is the market growth real and sustained, or cyclical/one-off? Forecast credibility starts with the market, not the spreadsheet.
3. **Assess the target's competitive position.** Relative share and trajectory, right-to-win, customer concentration, retention/churn, and win/loss reasons (`competitive-intel`, `customer-segmentation`). A target gaining share in a growing market is worth more than the same financials in a declining one.
4. **Stress-test the forecast.** Decompose management's projection into drivers; check each against market growth, the win rate implied, pipeline, and historical delivery-vs-plan. Identify the heroic assumptions (`assumption-audit`). Most deals are mispriced because the forecast was accepted, not tested.
5. **Surface red flags and deal-breakers.** Customer concentration, churn trends, channel/regulatory dependence, undisclosed competitive threats, key-person risk. State explicitly what would kill the deal and what the findings imply for price and terms.

## Key lenses
- Quality of growth = organic vs acquired, recurring vs one-time, new-logo vs price/volume — recurring organic growth deserves a higher multiple.
- Forecast credibility = implied win rate / share gain vs historical track record and market growth. If the plan needs a step-change with no mechanism, discount it.
- Customer concentration risk = % revenue from top 5/10 customers and their retention; high concentration caps the multiple.

## Decision rules
- Validate the forecast outside-in (does the market and competitive position *support* it?) before trusting the bottom-up build.
- Quality of earnings/growth matters as much as quantity — recurring, diversified, organic growth is worth materially more than the headline number.
- Tie every finding to price/terms or go/no-go. CDD that doesn't move the offer is academic.

## Output format
1. **Deal thesis & key questions** — why we're buying and what must be true.
2. **Market attractiveness** — size, growth, durability, profit pools.
3. **Competitive position** — share trajectory, right-to-win, retention, concentration.
4. **Forecast stress-test** — driver decomposition, credibility verdict, the heroic assumptions.
5. **Red flags & implications** — deal-breakers, and what the findings mean for price, terms, and go/no-go.

## Quality bar
- The forecast is independently rebuilt and tested, not restated from the data room.
- Outside-in market and competitive evidence, not just the target's own numbers.
- Findings tie to price/terms/go-no-go — a recommendation, not a description.
- Quality of growth and concentration risk are explicitly assessed.

## Common failure modes
- Accepting management's forecast and just reformatting it.
- Confirming the deal thesis the sponsor already loves (deal heat) instead of testing it.
- A market overview with no link to whether *this* target wins or what to pay.

## Pairs with
Built on `problem-structuring`, `market-mapping`, `profit-pool-analysis`, `competitive-intel`, and `assumption-audit`. Feeds `valuation` (the forecast you'll actually pay for), `synergy-valuation` (if a strategic buyer), and the `decision-memo` (the IC recommendation).

## Worked example
Input: a PE fund evaluating a $200M SaaS target growing 30%/yr. Thesis: buy durable recurring growth. Outside-in: the market grows 12%, so the target's 30% requires share gains — plausible only if win rates hold. Position: net revenue retention is strong (115%) but the top 5 customers are 48% of revenue and one is out for renewal next year. Forecast stress-test: management's plan implies win rates rising from 22% to 35% with no named mechanism — heroic. Verdict: growth quality is good (recurring, high NRR) but concentration is a real risk and the forecast is ~15% too aggressive. Implication: bid supports a strong multiple but priced on a haircut forecast, with an indemnity/earn-out tied to the at-risk top customer.
